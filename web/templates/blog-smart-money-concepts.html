Smart Money Concepts (SMC): FVG, BOS, CHoCH, and Order Blocks Explained

📅 April 21, 2026 ⏱️ ~11 min read ✍️ PiiTrade Team

Smart Money Concepts (SMC) refer to a framework for reading forex markets from the perspective of institutional traders — banks, hedge funds, central banks, and proprietary trading firms that collectively drive the vast majority of forex volume.

The core idea behind SMC is that large institutional players leave traceable footprints in price action as they build and unwind large positions. By learning to identify these patterns, retail traders can align themselves with — rather than against — the "smart money."

1. 📊 Fair Value Gap (FVG)

FVG

What is a Fair Value Gap?

A Fair Value Gap is a three-candle price imbalance pattern that occurs when price moves so rapidly in one direction that a "gap" is left between the upper wick of the first candle and the lower wick of the third candle (for a bullish FVG), or between the lower wick of the first candle and the upper wick of the third candle (for a bearish FVG).

The term "fair value" reflects the idea that markets tend to return to re-balance these inefficiencies — price often retraces back into an FVG before continuing in the original direction.

Bullish vs. Bearish FVG

  • Bullish FVG: Occurs during a sharp upward move. The gap is between candle 1's upper wick and candle 3's lower wick. Price often returns to fill this zone before continuing upward. Traders use bullish FVGs as potential buy zones on a pullback.
  • Bearish FVG: Occurs during a sharp downward move. The gap is between candle 1's lower wick and candle 3's upper wick. Price often retraces up into this zone before continuing lower. Traders use bearish FVGs as potential sell zones on a bounce.

2. 📈 Break of Structure (BOS)

BOS

What is a Break of Structure?

Break of Structure is the foundational concept in SMC market structure analysis. It identifies the moment when price breaks a previous significant swing high (in an uptrend) or swing low (in a downtrend), confirming that the existing trend is continuing.

In a healthy uptrend, price forms a series of Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). Each time price breaks above the most recent swing high, that is a Bullish BOS — confirmation that buyers are in control and the uptrend is intact.

In a downtrend, price forms Lower Lows (LL) and Lower Highs (LH). Each time price breaks below the most recent swing low, that is a Bearish BOS — confirmation sellers are in control.

Why BOS Matters for Trading

  • Trend confirmation: A BOS in your intended trade direction confirms you are trading with the trend, not against it.
  • Entry timing: After a bullish BOS, price often retraces (potentially into an FVG or order block) before continuing upward. This retracement provides a better risk-to-reward entry than chasing the breakout itself.
  • Target setting: The most recent swing high (for a BOS upward) can serve as an initial take-profit target, as it represents the next structural resistance level.

3. 🔄 Change of Character (CHoCH)

CHoCH

What is a Change of Character?

Change of Character is perhaps the most important SMC concept for traders seeking reversal opportunities. While BOS confirms the continuation of a trend, CHoCH signals a potential reversal — a shift in who controls the market.

A CHoCH occurs when price, in the context of an established uptrend, breaks below a previous significant swing low for the first time. This "breaks character" for the uptrend — markets in uptrend are not supposed to make lower lows. This first structural break downward is the CHoCH, signalling that sellers may be taking control.

Conversely, in a downtrend, a CHoCH occurs when price breaks above a previous swing high for the first time, suggesting buyers are stepping in.

CHoCH vs. BOS

  • BOS: Price breaks in the direction of the existing trend — trend continuation.
  • CHoCH: Price breaks against the existing trend — potential reversal signal.
⚠️ CHoCH alone is not a trade entry signal. It is an alert that the trend may be reversing. Wait for confirmation — typically a subsequent BOS in the new direction — before committing to a reversal trade. A single CHoCH can also be a temporary pullback in a strong trend.

4. 🏦 Order Blocks

Order Block

What is an Order Block?

An Order Block is the last candle (or group of candles) moving in the opposite direction before a strong impulsive move. The theory is that institutional traders place large "block" orders in this zone before executing the major move, and price often returns to this area to "fill" any unfilled institutional orders before continuing in the original direction.

In practice:

  • Bullish Order Block: The last bearish (red) candle body before a strong bullish impulsive move. This zone (from the open to the close of that candle) often becomes a support area when price retraces to it.
  • Bearish Order Block: The last bullish (green) candle body before a strong bearish impulsive move. This zone often acts as resistance when price retraces upward.

Why Order Blocks Work

Institutional traders cannot enter their full position in a single candle without moving the market against themselves. They gradually build positions. The order block represents an area where they accumulated their position. When price returns to that area, unfilled orders at that level are believed to absorb the retracement and push price back in the original direction.

Order Block vs. Support/Resistance

Order blocks are conceptually similar to horizontal support and resistance levels, but they are derived specifically from the structural context of institutional price action rather than simply horizontal price levels. Many traders find they produce cleaner, more precise zones with better risk-to-reward potential.

5. Using SMC Concepts Together: A Practical Framework

The power of SMC comes from combining these concepts into a coherent trading framework. Here is a simplified entry workflow using all four concepts:

  1. Identify the higher-timeframe trend using BOS. Is price making HH/HL (uptrend) or LH/LL (downtrend)?
  2. Wait for a CHoCH on a lower timeframe (e.g., 1H within a 4H uptrend) to signal a pullback is beginning.
  3. Identify a bullish order block or FVG in the direction of the higher-timeframe trend as the potential entry zone for the pullback.
  4. Wait for price to return to that zone and show signs of rejection (e.g., a bullish engulfing candle, a pin bar, or RSI recovering from oversold).
  5. Enter with stop below the order block/FVG and target the most recent swing high or the next significant BOS level.

This framework combines trend context, structure, and a precise entry zone — the hallmark of institutional-style trading.
